# Annual Report on Form 10-K — Item 1A. Risk Factors (excerpt)

**Synthetic 10-K excerpt · Fiscal year ended December 31, 2025**

## Risks related to our use of artificial intelligence and machine learning

We integrate generative artificial intelligence (AI) and machine
learning systems across our customer-facing operations, including
in our customer service, content moderation, financial fraud
detection, and product recommendation systems. Our use of these
technologies subjects us to a range of evolving risks that could
materially affect our financial condition and results of operations.

### Regulatory risk

Our AI and machine-learning systems operate in jurisdictions with
rapidly evolving regulatory frameworks, including the European
Union AI Act, the proposed United States Algorithmic Accountability
Act, and analogous legislation in California, Colorado, New York,
Texas, and Washington. Compliance with these frameworks may require
substantial changes to our model training, deployment, and
monitoring practices. We may be required to disclose information
about our models, training data, or operational metrics that we
currently treat as confidential or competitively sensitive.

In particular, the EU AI Act's provisions for "high-risk AI systems"
came into effect August 2026 and apply to several of our customer
service and fraud detection systems. We have invested approximately
$48 million during fiscal year 2025 in compliance infrastructure,
internal red-teaming, and third-party audit relationships. We expect
to invest a comparable amount in fiscal year 2026. There can be no
assurance that this investment will be sufficient to maintain
compliance, and any failure to comply could subject us to fines of
up to 7% of global annual revenue per violation under the EU AI Act.

### Operational and reputational risk

Generative AI systems can produce inaccurate, biased, or
inappropriate outputs, sometimes referred to as "hallucinations" or
"failure modes." During fiscal year 2025, we experienced two
notable incidents in which our customer service AI provided
inaccurate refund eligibility information to customers, resulting in
approximately $4.2 million in unanticipated refund obligations and
related customer-recovery costs. We have since deployed retrieval-
augmented generation (RAG) architectures and trust-aware response
filters to reduce the likelihood of recurrence, but we cannot
guarantee that similar incidents will not occur in the future.

### Adversarial and social-engineering risk

Our customer service operations have faced increasing attempts at
social engineering, including by external parties using AI-generated
content to impersonate authority figures, fabricate emergencies, or
manipulate our representatives into bypassing internal controls.
During fiscal year 2025, we observed a 340% year-over-year increase
in inbound communications exhibiting characteristics of AI-generated
social-engineering attempts. We have invested in cognitive-defense
training and real-time content scoring tools to mitigate this risk.

### Intellectual property risk

The training and operation of our AI systems may infringe the
intellectual property rights of third parties, including in
jurisdictions where the legal status of model training on
copyrighted material remains unsettled. We are subject to several
ongoing legal proceedings concerning these issues, the outcomes of
which we cannot predict.
